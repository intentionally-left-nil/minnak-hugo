The Wonderland Trail circumnavigates Mount Rainier — 93 miles, 22,000 feet of elevation gain. Attempting a section in January is a different endeavor entirely.

Most accounts of the Wonderland Trail are summer trip reports: permits, bear canisters, wildflower meadows at Spray Park. The winter version involves microspikes, whiteout navigation, and the particular silence of a snow-buried forest.

## The section

I skied the Ipsut Creek corridor to Mowich Lake over two days in late January. About 18 miles total, with a 1,200-foot climb to the lake. The road to the Ipsut Creek Campground is gated in winter, adding 5 miles of flat groomed surface before the trail narrows.

## What surprised me

The solitude is absolute. I saw no other people either day. The Carbon River campground, packed in summer, was unmarked — the entire site buried under three feet of snow. I found it by the bear boxes.

## The weather window

Pacific Northwest winter weather is the real planning constraint. You need a specific pattern: a high-pressure ridge after a storm cycle, temperatures above 20°F at trailhead elevation, and a second storm system at least 48 hours out. This happens maybe six times a winter, and forecasting accuracy drops sharply past 72 hours.

I watch the University of Washington atmospheric sciences models obsessively in January. Worth it.
