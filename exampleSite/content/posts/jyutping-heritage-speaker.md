Growing up speaking Cantonese at home but never learning to read or write it creates a strange linguistic gap. Here's how I've been closing it.

## The heritage speaker problem

I can hold a conversation with my grandparents. I understand the grocery store, family arguments, old Cantonese movies. But ask me to read a restaurant menu in traditional characters and I'm lost. Ask me to write a text message and I switch to English.

This is the heritage speaker experience: fluency without literacy.

## Why Jyutping matters

Jyutping is the romanization system for Cantonese developed by the Linguistic Society of Hong Kong in 1993. Learning it properly — including the six tones — gave me a way to anchor my spoken knowledge to a written form I could look up, annotate, and remember.

The tones are the hardest part. Cantonese has more tones than Mandarin (six full tones, sometimes counted as nine with the checked tones), and my native-speaker intuition, while correct, was hard to consciously articulate.

## Tools that helped

- **Cantonese.wiki** — searchable database of Jyutping pronunciations
- **Pleco** — dictionary with audio, used daily
- **HelloTalk** — language exchange with actual Cantonese speakers from Hong Kong

The biggest unlock was finding a tutor from Hong Kong who could explain the logic behind written Cantonese versus standard written Chinese. Once I understood the code-switching patterns, reading became much less intimidating.
